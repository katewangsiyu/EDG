\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:Introduction}

Machine learning force fields (MLFF) can predict molecular energies and forces with near-quantum accuracy at a fraction of the cost of ab initio methods~\cite{behler2007generalized,unke2021machine,chmiela2017machine,xiang2024molecular,wang2024efficiently}. Their success stems largely from geometric deep learning architectures that respect E(3) symmetries~\cite{thomas2018tensor,fuchs2020se3}, representing atoms as nodes in geometric graphs~\cite{liu2022spherical,wang2024enhancing}. However, existing methods operate at the atomic level---modeling coordinates, multi-body interactions, \etc~\cite{batzner20223,liao2023equiformer,wang2024enhancing}---without explicitly incorporating information at the electronic level.

% [Polish] Original: Electron density (ED) is a core quantum mechanical property describing the distribution of electrons within a molecule and is crucial for accurately predicting quantum chemical properties...underscoring ED's central role in molecular physics.
Electron density (ED) describes how electrons are distributed within a molecule and is central to predicting quantum chemical properties~\cite{sunshine2023chemical,skogh2024electron}. \emph{ED is not merely another view of geometry, but the physical manifestation of chemical bonding, polarization, conjugation, and lone-pair effects; many quantum properties are direct functions of the electronic structure.} Nuclear positions dictate the ED, which in turn determines observables such as total energy, forces, and electrostatic potentials. In density functional theory (DFT)~\cite{kohn1965self}, the Hohenberg-Kohn theorem~\cite{hohenberg1964inhomogeneous} formalizes this: the ground-state energy is a unique functional of the electron density.

% [Polish] Original: From an information-theoretic perspective, ED can be viewed as an approximate latent mediator...enabling them to exhibit electron-level inductive bias while preserving their symmetry guarantees at inference.
From an information-theoretic perspective, ED acts as an \emph{approximate latent mediator}: geometry $\rightarrow$ electron density $\rightarrow$ property. The Hohenberg-Kohn theorem supports this view by establishing ground-state energy as a unique functional of the electron density under fixed composition and charge. Standard geometry GNNs implicitly learn this mediating relationship from data, but may lack sufficient inductive bias. Our approach explicitly provides the mediator's structure through DFT-computed ED during training, then distills this knowledge back to geometry models. A further challenge is the symmetry gap between modalities: geometry GNNs enforce E(3) equivariance, whereas ED images from 2D projections of 3D density fields rely on multi-view rendering to approximate rotational invariance. Our distillation framework addresses this: \emph{we transfer electronic structure knowledge from a non-equivariant visual teacher to equivariant geometry students, so that they acquire electron-level inductive bias while retaining symmetry guarantees at inference}.

The application of ED faces three major challenges:

\textit{Challenge 1: High computational complexity of ED.} Unlike the number of atoms in a molecule, which is usually on the scale of tens to hundreds, the ED relies on a continuous spatial distribution and as the resolution increases, the number of data points may reach millions or even tens of millions. As shown in Fig.~\ref{Fig1_Intro}(a), there are two direct ways to represent ED: point cloud~\cite{guo2020deep} and voxel~\cite{gong2023incorporation}. As shown in Fig.~\ref{Fig1_Intro}(b), we empirically show the limitations of point clouds and voxels as ED representations in energy prediction, GPU memory efficiency, and training efficiency. In particular, point clouds and voxels are directly related to the resolution of the ED, so the computational efficiency will decrease as the resolution increases. These limitations motivate us to propose a multi-view RGB-D image (the right subfigure of Fig.~\ref{Fig1_Intro}(a)) for accurate and efficient ED representation, which produces a fixed-size output regardless of ED grid resolution and compresses the continuous ED signal into pixels. Compared with point clouds and voxels, the proposed images improve energy prediction ability, GPU memory efficiency, and training efficiency by 38.4\%, 42.1\%, and 4.8\%, respectively.

\textit{Challenge 2: Expensive ED acquisition.} Obtaining ED data requires either costly experimental techniques (\eg, X-ray crystallography~\cite{nienaber2000discovering}, neutron diffraction~\cite{goncharenko2005neutron}) or computationally intensive quantum chemical calculations (\eg, DFT~\cite{kohn1965self,hegde2017machine,lee2024convolutional}), both of which are resource-intensive. As shown in Fig.~\ref{Fig1_Intro}(c), we address this by transforming ED-enhanced geometry learning into a teacher-student paradigm. We first use DFT to obtain 2 million high-quality ED data and train an ED representation learning model (ImageED). We then transfer the knowledge in ImageED to an ED-aware teacher that takes structural images (without ED) as input, which subsequently distills geometry students. This scheme, called \textbf{EDG} (Electron Density-enhanced Geometry learning), requires ED computation only once during pre-training, eliminating the need for ED data in all subsequent stages. As shown in Fig.~\ref{Fig1_Intro}(d), geometry students equipped with EDG and EDG++ achieve significant prediction improvements on QM9.

\textit{Challenge 3: Unreliable teacher predictions causing negative transfer.} Beyond feasibility and efficiency, a critical question remains: \emph{when should we trust the teacher's predictions?} The quality of cross-modal predictions varies across samples due to intrinsic DFT approximations, molecular conformation diversity, and information loss when compressing 3D electron clouds into 2D images. \emph{Naive distillation implicitly assumes uniform teacher fidelity, risking negative transfer}~\cite{pan2010survey}. To address this, we propose \textbf{EDG++}, a reliability-aware extension that introduces a pre-trained reliability estimator and an adaptive global-local thresholding mechanism for selective distillation, filtering unreliable teacher predictions before knowledge transfer (Section~\ref{sec:Theoretical_Analysis}--\ref{sec:Adaptive_Thresholding}).

We summarize the main contributions as follows:
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=2pt,topsep=0pt,parsep=0pt]
    \item We propose EDG, an electron density distillation framework that injects electronic structure inductive bias into geometry-based models without adding inference cost, using electron density as privileged information available only during training.
    \item We design an efficient multi-view RGB-D image representation for electron density and develop ImageED, a masked autoencoder that learns ED-related features, along with an ED-aware teacher that transfers this knowledge without requiring ED at inference.
    \item We extend EDG to EDG++ by introducing a pre-trained reliability estimator and an adaptive thresholding mechanism combining global and local statistics, enabling selective distillation to mitigate negative transfer. We provide a theoretical analysis establishing a sufficient condition under which selective distillation reduces an upper bound on gradient noise, supported by empirical observation of heavy-tailed error structures.
    \item We demonstrate improvements on the majority of tasks across multiple architectures on QM9 (SchNet, Equiformer, SphereNet) and rMD17 (SphereNet with EDG++; SchNet, ViSNet with EDG), achieving gains on 34/36 QM9 task-model combinations and 17/20 rMD17 energy/force predictions, while characterizing the boundary conditions of the approach.
\end{itemize}
