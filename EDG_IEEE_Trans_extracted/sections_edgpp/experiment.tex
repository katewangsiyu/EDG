\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:Experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Settings}

\textbf{Datasets and evaluation protocol.} To pre-train ImageED, the ED-aware teacher, the ED predictor, and the reliability estimator, we select the first 2 million unlabeled molecular conformations and their DFT-computed ED data from the EDBench database~\cite{xiang2025edbenchlargescaleelectrondensity}, generated by Psi4 software~\cite{turney2012psi4} with a grid spacing of 0.4 Bohr. In the evaluation stage, we select 12 widely used tasks related to quantum mechanical properties from QM9~\cite{ramakrishnan2014quantum} and 10 common tasks related to energy/force from revised MD17 (rMD17)~\cite{christensen2020role}. For force prediction, we first predict the molecular energy and use the gradient of each node position as the force, \ie, \textit{force=-torch.autograd.grad(outputs=energy, inputs=positions)} in PyTorch~\cite{paszke2019pytorch}. The dataset split follows Geom3D~\cite{liu2024symmetry}: 110K for training, 10K for validation, and 11K for testing in QM9; 950 for training, 50 for validation, and 1000 for testing in rMD17. We use mean absolute error (MAE) as the evaluation metric.

% [Polish] Original: To verify the effectiveness of EDG and EDG++, we select geometry-based models
\textbf{Baselines.} To evaluate EDG and EDG++, we select geometry-based models with diverse architectures as geometry students. For QM9, we evaluate SchNet~\cite{schutt2017schnet}, Equiformer~\cite{liao2023equiformer}, and SphereNet~\cite{liu2022spherical}. For rMD17, we additionally evaluate ViSNet~\cite{wang2024enhancing} with EDG, and conduct EDG++ experiments on SphereNet. Following~\cite{liu2022spherical,wang2024enhancing,liu2024symmetry}, we ensure that each baseline is fully trained with the same hyperparameters as in the original papers.

\textbf{Implementation details.} The implementation details of ImageED pre-training, ED-aware teacher pre-training, and reliability estimator training are provided in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_implementation}. In the distillation stage, we conduct grid search over the hyperparameters described in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_hyperparam}. The mapper $f_M$ and task predictor $f_T$ each consist of a single linear layer. We select the best checkpoint based on validation MAE for QM9 and validation force MAE for rMD17.

\textbf{Hyperparameter search and evaluation protocol.} We follow a strict protocol to avoid data leakage: hyperparameter search is performed exclusively on the validation set, while all reported results are evaluated on the held-out test set. For EDG++ results, we report the best result for each metric independently across all hyperparameter configurations (per-task-best), which provides an \emph{optimistic upper bound} on what the method can achieve when task-specific tuning is feasible. For practical deployment, a single shared configuration should be selected; the default-threshold ($\kappa\!=\!0$) configuration already captures the majority of the improvement (Table~\ref{tab:incremental}). EDG baselines are evaluated with the best $\lambda$ from the same grid, ensuring a fair comparison on the hyperparameter search budget.

\textbf{Single-seed protocol.} All experiments use a single random seed (seed=42). We assess statistical reliability via: (1)~paired bootstrap CIs on per-sample test predictions (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_bootstrap}); (2)~sign tests (34/36 QM9 wins: $p < 10^{-8}$; 17/20 rMD17: $p\!=\!0.001$); and (3)~coefficient of variation across 45 configurations (Section~\ref{sec:model_robustness}). Multi-seed evaluation remains a limitation (Section~\ref{sec:Conclusion}).

Table~\ref{tab:qm9_edgpp} presents the main QM9 results. EDG++ improves 34 out of 36 task-model combinations (sign test $p < 10^{-8}$), with average relative gains of 3.2\% (SchNet), 9.0\% (Equiformer), and 3.6\% (SphereNet). These are per-task-best results; the default-threshold ($\kappa\!=\!0$) configuration captures most of the gains (Table~\ref{tab:incremental}). Comparing EDG++ with EDG, selective distillation outperforms naive distillation on the majority of tasks (SchNet: 10/12, SphereNet: 7/12), confirming that reliability-aware filtering successfully excludes unreliable teacher predictions.

\textbf{Property-dependent improvements.} The improvements exhibit a physically interpretable hierarchy consistent with the Hohenberg-Kohn theorem: thermodynamic properties achieve the largest and most consistent improvements (5/5 win rate across all architectures; avg.\ +3.70\% to +16.30\%), as these ground-state observables are uniquely determined by the electron density~\cite{hohenberg1964inhomogeneous}. The only two degradation cases---Equiformer on $\mu$ ($\downarrow$1.9\%) and ZPVE ($\downarrow$3.2\%)---are architecture-specific and improve on SchNet and SphereNet, confirming this is not a fundamental limitation. Figure~\ref{fig:property_group} visualizes this hierarchy across all 36 combinations; the detailed property-group breakdown is in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_property_group}.

Tables~\ref{tab:rmd17_energy} and~\ref{tab:rmd17_force} present the rMD17 results. EDG achieves consistent energy improvements across all architectures (SchNet: 33.7\%, SphereNet: 25.2\% average), with more modest force improvements (1.5--5.3\%), as force depends on local atomic interactions rather than the global energy distribution captured by ED. Note that EDG++ experiments on rMD17 are conducted on SphereNet only due to computational constraints; the cross-architecture generality of EDG++ is established on QM9 (three architectures), while rMD17 provides a single-architecture case study with molecule-level analysis. We note that rMD17 uses only 50 validation samples, so per-molecule threshold tuning carries higher overfitting risk than on QM9.

\textbf{Progressive improvement from EDG to EDG++.} To isolate the reliability evaluator's contribution, we introduce a controlled baseline: EDG ($\lambda\!=\!0.001$) applies naive distillation with the same $\lambda$ as EDG++ but \emph{without} the reliability estimator. Comparing EDG ($\lambda\!=\!0.001$) $\to$ EDG++ ($\kappa\!=\!0$), which differs \emph{only} in whether the evaluator is active, the evaluator improves energy on 8/10 molecules, with naphthalene ($\downarrow$54.5\%), salicylic acid ($\downarrow$32.4\%), and aspirin ($\downarrow$30.7\%) showing the largest gains. Notably, naive distillation at $\lambda\!=\!0.001$ without the evaluator \emph{degrades} energy below baseline on 4/10 molecules (e.g., naphthalene: $\uparrow$36.4\%); the evaluator recovers 3 of these 4 cases to below-baseline error, demonstrating that reliability-aware filtering is essential for safe distillation at this weight. Threshold tuning (EDG++ $\kappa\!=\!0$ $\to$ EDG++ best $\kappa$) provides further incremental gains. Overall, EDG++ achieves the best energy on 7/10 and best force on 4/10 molecules; the smaller force gains are expected, as force depends on local atomic interactions rather than the global energy distribution captured by ED.

\textbf{Malonaldehyde: a boundary case.} Malonaldehyde is the only molecule where the reliability estimator consistently degrades performance. Instructively, naive distillation at $\lambda\!=\!0.001$ \emph{does} improve energy (0.05806 vs.\ baseline 0.06005, $\downarrow$3.3\%), indicating that the ED-based distillation signal is beneficial. However, activating the evaluator (EDG++ $\kappa\!=\!0$: 0.06767) reverses this gain, suggesting that the evaluator incorrectly filters beneficial distillation signal for this molecule---likely because malonaldehyde's rapid intramolecular proton transfer dynamics produce ED patterns that the evaluator misclassifies as unreliable. EDG with per-task-best $\lambda$ achieves the best result (0.05659), and threshold tuning partially mitigates the degradation (0.06611 vs.\ 0.06767 at $\kappa{=}0$), pointing to molecule-adaptive thresholding as a future direction. A per-sample error visualization is provided in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_rmd17_vis}; additional sensitivity analysis is in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_heatmap}.

\subsubsection{Hyperparameter Sensitivity}
\label{sec:model_robustness}

The distillation weight $\lambda$ exhibits architecture-dependent sensitivity (detailed per-task results in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_lambda}): Equiformer averages 7.77\% sensitivity (5$\times$ SchNet's 1.47\%), with thermodynamic properties most sensitive ($U_0$: 34.87\%). SchNet maintains consistently low sensitivity (max 2.37\%). The coefficient of variation (CV) of MAE across all 45 configurations further confirms robustness: SchNet (mean CV = 1.1\%), SphereNet (1.8\%), and Equiformer (4.21\%). Even Equiformer's default-threshold ($\kappa\!=\!0$) captures the majority of the improvement (Table~\ref{tab:incremental}). Additional hyperparameter interaction analysis is in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_heatmap}.

\subsection{Advantages of ED Image Representation}

As shown in Fig.~\ref{Fig1_Intro}(b), the proposed multi-view RGB-D image representation outperforms point cloud and voxel alternatives on all 6 energy prediction tasks (7.8--71.6\% relative RMSE gains on 10,000 molecules with an 8:1:1 split), while producing a fixed-size output independent of source grid resolution. Detailed per-task RMSE values are provided in Table~\ref{tab:results_ED_images} (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_ed_repr}). Qualitative visualization of ImageED reconstruction quality is provided in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_imageed_vis}.
